Is AAPI Data
Misleading?
In current statistics, the AAPI (Asian American Pacific Islander) community is categorized as a whole under the group "Asian Americans." This generalization ignores the unique differences of each racial group that make up Asian Americans.

What's the problem?

We are lumping together groups that are clearly different!


AAPI (Asian American Pacific Islander) data groups all members of the AAPI community as simply "Asians Americans." Because of this, the unique differences are each group is overlooked. Hiding this information masks the issues that specific AAPI groups face.

Why does this matter?

Reinforces the Model Minority

Current data that only accounts for Asian Americans as a whole disregards the issues that individual AAPI groups face. It reinforces the model minority, the false concept that Asian Americans are the most successful racial minority group socioeconomically.


Potential for Political Change

Correct data will allow for policy makers to accurately target legislation for communities in need.



Instrumental to Public Health

There are significant differences among Asian Americans when it comes to risks for diseases like Hepatitis B, cancer, and cardiovascular disease. These differences are worth noting in AAPI data so that health educators can focus on issues that matter.



What is a possible solution?

Disaggregate AAPI Data


Asian Americans are the fastest growing racial group in the country. They are growing over 4 times as fast as the entire U.S population.Yet, Asian Americans are one of the most understudied racial groups in the U.S. Disaggregating data has the potential to result in more research to better serve the needs of the AAPI community.


It's been done!


On September 26, 2016, California passed a bill called Accounting for Health and Education in API Demographics (AHEAD) Act. This bill will mandate the state Department of Public Health to break down data it collects by ethnicity or ancestry for AAPI groups.
